The no-show she couldn't charge

She'd done everything right. Confirmed the booking the day before. Sent the location pin. Even moved her 11am to fit the client in. And at 2:14pm, fourteen minutes into a billed hour, she finally accepted what the silence already knew — the woman wasn't coming.

No call. No message. No apology in the next 48 hours either. Just a quiet, expensive disappearance.

The R450 wasn't the problem. The problem was the hour. She'd turned down a paying client to hold that slot. She'd prepped. She'd shown up. And there was no contract, no card on file, no deposit — nothing in the world to make the no-show cost the no-show anything at all.

So she did what most service founders I work with do: she sent a polite follow-up, got ghosted, and quietly wrote it off. Then she did the more dangerous thing — she carried the resentment into the next three bookings, second-guessing every new client, wondering which one would do it next.

"I want to charge a deposit. I just don't know how to ask for one without sounding like I don't trust them."

That sentence is the whole problem in one breath. And it lives at the intersection of three things every founder I've ever sat with eventually trips over:

  • The timing problem — when do you introduce a deposit policy without losing the booking on the spot?
  • The precedent problem — your first five clients didn't pay one, so how do you start now without insulting the next five?
  • The justification problem — you know why you need it, but every script you draft sounds defensive, apologetic, or vaguely accusatory.

Here's the reframe that broke the loop for her, and that I want to offer you if you're sitting on the same fence: a deposit is not a trust test. It is a calendar lock. You're not asking the client to prove they're not a flake. You're asking them to hold their own chair at the table — because if they don't, you can't sell it to someone else in time.

Once you say it that way — out loud, to a real client — the conversation stops being about character and starts being about logistics. Logistics, every adult understands.

She rewrote her booking page that weekend. Two lines. A 30% deposit, refundable up to 24 hours before. The next no-show cost the no-show. The one after that didn't happen at all.

If you're still reading and you don't yet have a deposit policy — that's the assignment this week. Two lines. Send it to me, I'll workshop it with you.
