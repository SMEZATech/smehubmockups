Guide to Asset Finance in South Africa

Few things stall a growing South African business faster than a lack of equipment. Asset finance lets you put the vehicles, machinery and tools you need to work today — and pay for them out of the income they help you earn, rather than from cash you don't have.

What is asset finance

Asset finance is a type of lending used to acquire a specific, tangible business asset — a delivery vehicle, a piece of plant, kitchen equipment, or IT hardware — where the asset itself usually serves as security for the loan. Because the lender's risk is tied to something they can repossess and resell, asset finance is often easier to access than an unsecured loan, especially for younger businesses.

It is offered by banks, specialist asset financiers and, increasingly, by the equipment suppliers themselves. For most SMEs it takes one of a few common forms covered below.

How it works

In practice, the financier pays the supplier for the asset and you repay them over an agreed term — typically 12 to 60 months — in fixed monthly instalments. The structure determines who owns the asset and when:

  • Instalment sale / hire purchase — you own the asset from day one and it appears on your balance sheet; ownership simply transfers free of the financier's interest once the final instalment clears.
  • Finance lease — the financier owns the asset and leases it to you; you carry it on your books and usually buy it for a nominal amount at the end.
  • Operating lease / rental — you effectively rent the asset for a period and hand it back, useful for fast-depreciating equipment you'll want to upgrade.

Who qualifies

Asset financiers look at both your business and the asset. A strong, in-demand asset that holds its value can offset a thinner trading history. Typically you'll need:

  • A registered business with a CIPC number and a business bank account
  • Six to twelve months of bank statements showing cash flow that comfortably covers the instalment
  • An acceptable credit profile for the business and, often, its directors
  • A deposit — frequently 10% to 20% of the asset value

Costs & rates

The headline number is the interest rate, usually quoted as a margin over the prime lending rate. But the true cost of an asset finance deal is the sum of several parts:

  • Interest — linked to prime; lower for strong credit, larger deposits and shorter terms.
  • Initiation and admin fees — a once-off setup cost plus a small monthly service fee.
  • Balloon or residual — a lump sum deferred to the end to lower instalments, which you then settle or refinance.
  • Insurance — comprehensive cover on the asset is almost always mandatory.

Always compare offers on total cost of credit over the full term, not just the monthly figure — a low instalment with a large balloon can cost far more overall.

Pros & cons

Asset finance is a powerful tool, but it isn't free money. Weigh it honestly:

  • Pros — preserves working capital, spreads cost over the asset's useful life, often tax-efficient, and easier to obtain than unsecured credit.
  • Cons — total cost exceeds paying cash, the asset can be repossessed on default, and financing a depreciating asset over too long a term can leave you owing more than it's worth.

How to apply

The application itself is straightforward once your paperwork is in order. Work through these steps:

  1. Identify the exact asset and obtain a formal quote from the supplier.
  2. Gather your documents — company registration, ID of directors, and six to twelve months of bank statements.
  3. Decide on a deposit amount and the term that keeps instalments affordable.
  4. Approach two or three financiers (your bank plus a specialist) and request written offers.
  5. Compare the offers on total cost of credit, fees and any balloon, not the monthly instalment alone.
  6. Accept, sign the agreement, and the financier settles the supplier directly.
